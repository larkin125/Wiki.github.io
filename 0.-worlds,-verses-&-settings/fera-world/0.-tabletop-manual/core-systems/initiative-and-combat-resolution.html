The Planning Phase

Unlike many tabletop roleplaying games, combat in Fera does not operate on a traditional turn-based initiative system. Combat is intended to represent the confusion, danger, and unpredictability of real violence, where all participants are acting simultaneously rather than waiting for their turn.

During combat, all characters and creatures are considered to be acting within the same brief span of time.

At the beginning of each combat round, the Game Master will describe the current situation and any significant changes to the battlefield.

Players are then given a short period, typically between five and ten seconds, to decide upon their actions for the round.

Actions should be declared clearly and concisely. Examples include:

  • "I charge the nearest bandit."
  • "I fire my pistol at the man behind the cart."
  • "I move behind cover and reload."
  • "I cast a ward upon Thyra."
  • "I hold my ground and defend."

Once an action has been declared, it is considered committed.

Committed Actions

A declared action may not be changed once the Planning Phase has ended.

Characters are assumed to be making decisions under pressure, often with incomplete information and little time to react. A combatant cannot wait to see the outcome of another action before deciding upon their own.

This applies equally to player characters, allies, and enemies.

Simultaneous Resolution

After all actions have been declared, the Game Master resolves the round.

All actions are considered to occur simultaneously unless a specific ability, weapon, skill, or rule states otherwise.

As a result:

  • Two combatants may wound one another in the same round.
  • A dying character may still complete an attack they committed to before receiving their injury.
  • A spellcaster may complete a spell while suffering a wound inflicted during the same round.
  • Multiple combatants may attempt to occupy the same position or engage the same target.

Combat should be resolved as a sequence of overlapping events rather than a strict order of actions.

Interruption and Prevention

Although actions occur simultaneously, certain actions may prevent, negate, or reduce the effects of other actions.

Examples include:

  • A successful parry deflecting a melee attack.
  • A shield blocking an incoming strike.
  • A dodge causing an attack to miss.
  • A magical ward absorbing damage.
  • A combatant taking cover before a projectile reaches its target.

These actions do not occur before another combatant's turn. Instead, they represent reactions and defensive measures occurring during the same combat interval.

Unintended Consequences

The chaos of battle can produce unexpected outcomes.

Missed attacks, deflected blows, ricochets, magical mishaps, and shifting positions may result in attacks striking unintended targets.

The Game Master may determine that a failed attack, particularly one involving ranged weapons, explosives, or powerful magic, affects another combatant or object within the area.

Combatants should therefore consider their positioning carefully, especially when allies are nearby.

Combat Rounds

Each combat round represents several seconds of frantic action.

A combat round is resolved using the following sequence:

  1. The Game Master describes the battlefield.
  2. All participants decide upon their actions.
  3. Actions are declared.
  4. Actions become committed.
  5. The Game Master resolves all actions simultaneously.
  6. The consequences of the round are applied.
  7. A new round begins.

This process continues until combat has concluded.

The Nature of Combat

Combat in Fera is intended to be dangerous, fast-paced, and unpredictable.

Success is determined not only by skill and equipment, but by judgement, positioning, timing, and a willingness to accept risk. Every action carries consequences, and hesitation can be as deadly as recklessness.
