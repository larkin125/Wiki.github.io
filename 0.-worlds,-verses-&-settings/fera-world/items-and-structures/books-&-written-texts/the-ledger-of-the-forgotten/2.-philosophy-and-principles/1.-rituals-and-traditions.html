1. Rituals and Traditions

Rituals and Traditions During Initiation

Unity Above All

The Children of the Abandoned, in all their wisdom and steadfastness, believe with unwavering conviction that their strength lies not in the individual, but in the collective. It is within the unity of the group that they find their resilience, their power, and their ultimate purpose. The story of their origin is one of sacrifice, of a knight abandoning personal glory to raise the forsaken. Thus, in their fellowship, personal needs and desires fall behind the communal good, for only by putting the collective above all can the Children truly endure.

In Eryndor’s words, "The strongest walls are those built by many hands." This core philosophy shapes their every action, forging their bonds as securely as the stones of a mighty fortress. Each member of the Children carries the weight of the group’s well-being on their shoulders—every action, whether small or great, must be made for the good of the whole. Sacrifice is not merely a concept to them—it is a way of life. To act for personal gain, to seek recognition or fame, is to betray the very principles upon which the Children stand.

Initiates are taught to surrender their egos long before the Naming Ceremony, learning that their worth is not found in recognition but in their contribution to the unity of the group. From the moment they enter the fold, they are reminded in quiet whispers of the words that started it all:

"We are not the sum of our faces, our names, or our past. We are the sum of our unity. Alone, we are fragile. Together, we are indomitable."

Through their actions, both small and grand, initiates learn the profound truth that, in this fellowship, one’s power lies in one’s service to the collective. They do not seek fame nor glory; their purpose is not in what they achieve as individuals, but in how their work supports those around them.

Discipline and Anonymity

The helmet is the Children’s most sacred symbol, a physical representation of their core belief: that unity demands equality, and equality demands the absence of ego. Upon the head of each initiate, the helmet ensures that their identity remains concealed, a constant reminder that they, like all members, are equal in silence and shared purpose. The face, once a symbol of distinction, is buried beneath the weight of the mask, where no one member may be identified or celebrated over the other.

In the stillness that pervades their lives, the Children recite, with quiet fervor, the chant that encapsulates their philosophical roots:

"In the hollow, we are one. In the silence, we are eternal."

The helmet becomes not only a shield but a crucible in which personal desires, ambitions, and failures are melted away to form something greater: collective identity. The face is not a mark of distinction or the source of one’s power; it is merely a mask worn by the world. Underneath, in silence and unity, they remain timeless—together, without end.

In meditation, the Children return often to their creed, which rings out in quiet verses:

"The face is the mark of vanity. The helmet bears the face of eternity. When the voice is silenced, the heart speaks with the full weight of the whole."

Through this meditative passage, initiates are urged to think beyond their individual identity. When they wear the helmet, they shed any traces of ego and begin to speak the language of the collective through actions rather than words. To remove the helmet is to break the bond, to commit the ultimate sin of self-promotion, an act that severally diminishes the strength of the entire group.

The Code of the Helmet

The Helmet is the greatest symbol of the Children of the Abandoned—it represents equality, discipline, and the ultimate devotion to anonymity. Wearing the helmet is not simply an act of concealment, but a religious and philosophical practice that shapes the very identity of each member.

In the eyes of the Children, there is nothing more sacred than this act of humble submission. To wear the helmet is to shed all pride, all ego. It serves as a physical and symbolic shield from the world outside—protecting them from the dangers of vanity and self-centeredness. To the Children, a helmet worn with reverence is a reflection of their vow to never allow individuality to rise above the spirit of the group.

Removing the helmet, particularly in the presence of another member, is an unforgivable sin. It is a violation of their sacred law—a breaking of trust and unity that cannot be undone. Such an act strips one of their standing in the group, marking them forever as an outsider. For no matter the past glory or accomplishments of the individual, once the helmet has been removed before another, they are rendered vulnerable to the very thing they vowed to forsake—the dangers of being seen.

The Helm of Naming is a critical rite of passage for initiates—the moment they assume their place as a member of the whole. The passage they must take, from The Forgotten to The Named, requires they wear the helmet at all times, as they are no longer individuals but reflections of the group as a whole.

In the sacred texts and whispered teachings, the motto of the Children is clear:

"The face is a mask worn by the world. But under the helmet, the heart beats as one."

This passage serves as both instruction and warning: The face, often associated with distinction or pride, is hollow beneath the helmet. Under the cover of anonymity, the individual becomes part of a much greater, unified collective—a purpose that supersedes everything. Each act is measured by its service to the group, not by the desire to be seen or recognized, but by how it strengthens the unbreakable walls forged through unity.

The focus on self-sacrifice, discipline, and the sacredness of the helmet helps solidify both the spiritual and physical facets of the group's identity, reminding initiates at every step that they are bound, not to their personal name or face, but to the legacy of Eryndor and the collective whole of the fellowship.
