1. The Naming Ceremony

The Naming Ceremony: A Rite of Rebirth and Solidarity

In the Children of the Abandoned, the act of naming is not just a tradition; it is a sacred rite that marks the transition of a nameless child from the forsaken into a full, valued member of the group. To be named is to be reborn. It is a moment that severs the ties to the past and anchors them firmly into the present, into the fellowship of those who have embraced the same creed.

The Calling of the Forgotten

When a new child arrives among the Children, whether through adoption or as an orphan seeking refuge, they enter the fold without a name. They are known simply as The Forgotten, a title that reminds them of their past—of their abandonment, their struggles, and the harsh world they once inhabited. This name is not spoken aloud but instead passed on by the group through actions and gestures. The Forgotten child receives no individual recognition, no title, until they have earned it through their commitment, discipline, and unity.

During the time they are called The Forgotten, they are taught the deep philosophy of anonymity and solidarity. It is a time of learning, a time of humble service and silent dedication to the group's needs. They are not given the luxury of individual recognition during this time; their work reflects the effort of the group as a whole, reinforcing that no one is above another, no one is greater than the collective. A Forgotten does not eat before others, nor do they receive praise. This period can last anywhere from months to years, depending on the individual’s transformation.

Preparation for the Ceremony

As a Forgotten child reaches the precipice of maturity, a moment will come when they demonstrate exceptional dedication to the group’s philosophy—a deep understanding of the balance between selflessness and collective purpose, mastery over their emotions and impulses, and complete adherence to the teachings of Eryndor.

When a child demonstrates these qualities, the Elders observe quietly, waiting for the right moment to initiate the Naming Ceremony. It is always a quiet, almost reverential moment—a signal that the child is ready for this spiritual milestone. The child’s readiness is further marked by their proficiency in the sacred art of silence, their acceptance of the group’s unified way of living, and their complete rejection of any personal desire for fame or acknowledgement.

Before the Naming Ceremony can take place, the child undergoes an Initiation of Silence. For one full day, the initiate is separated from the group and placed in a secluded chamber, free from distractions or the temptations of speaking. In solitude, the initiate reflects on the teachings, sitting in stillness to meditate, a solemn meditation on their journey, their transformation from The Forgotten to a full member of the Children of the Abandoned.

On the evening of this solitary day, the child returns to the group to await the ceremony. They stand at the threshold of what they once were, with nothing more than their quiet resolve. The helmet, which has long been their companion and constant reminder of their submission to the fellowship, is carefully placed on their head before they are led to the ceremonial chamber.

The Ceremonial Space

The Naming Ceremony takes place in a specially prepared area known only to the Children, often secluded within sacred ruins, or deep within the fortress of their hideout, shielded from any outsider eyes. This space is marked by ancient symbols carved into stone, depicting the unity of the forgotten, and the commitment of each member to rise together as one. The space is simple but carries an otherworldly air—low stone walls dimly lit by candles flickering like the fragile hopes of those once forsaken by society.

At the center of the room lies an altar of sorts, where sacred items are kept, relics that tie the Children to their past and their founding. This altar is flanked by the Elders—those most revered within the group, the living bearers of the original oath sworn by Eryndor. Their faces remain hidden behind their own helmets, yet their presence is undeniable, their long-standing commitment to the group reflected in the care of their robes and the composure of their posture.

The initiate is led into the center of the ceremony, a place marked by unspoken expectation and quiet reverence. Here, in the flickering candlelight, the Helm of Naming is placed at the altar—a special ceremonial helmet made from the finest, purest metal. This helmet has no markings of distinction or personal style. It is blank, a symbol of the fact that in this moment, the initiate does not enter the group with the weight of past history or identity, but as an empty vessel, awaiting transformation.

The Silent Vow

Before the initiate can receive their new name, they must first take part in the Silent Vow. Standing before the altar, in front of their fellow children, they must place their hands upon the edge of the ceremonial helmet, eyes cast downward, silently repeating in their heart the sacred vow of unity:

"I forsake the world of individuals. I cast aside my name and face, that I may rise with those whose faces are hidden. In their shadows, I stand united. In their silence, I speak."

Once the vow has been silently spoken, the initiate remains still, unblinking, awaiting the call of the Elder who presides over the ceremony. A moment passes in complete silence—an agonizing yet sacred stillness, where time itself seems to slow in respect of the change that is to take place.

The Calling of the Name

At last, the silence breaks with a single word, spoken with deep reverence: "Arise."

This is the signal that the initiate is ready to receive their name, a name chosen from among the Elders after a period of careful observation and thought. No member can choose their own name—nor can they carry any of the names they once knew. The name must be chosen for the initiate based on their actions, their spirit, and the purpose they serve within the fellowship.

One of the Elders approaches, their voice barely audible yet clear, speaking the name. It is always an apt reflection of the individual’s true calling within the group—often inspired by something personal in their journey. The name is not always a traditional one, but is often more symbolic or abstract: "Sable," for one who has mastered the silence of shadow, or "Vigil," for one who stands guard over the principles of the collective.

At the uttering of this name, the initiate’s helmet is exchanged with the ceremonial helmet, now symbolic of the final act of rebirth. They place the helmet firmly upon their head, sealing their identity not as an individual, but as a member of the whole.

A wave of respect ripples through the gathered Children. There is no applause, no fanfare—just a unified exhale of acceptance and solidarity, as all stand, motionless, to silently acknowledge the change that has taken place.

The Oath of Solidarity

The final act of the ceremony is an act of joining. At the moment the initiate dons the helmet, they pledge the Oath of Solidarity. They step forward to each Elder, each member of the group, and silently exchange a moment of connection. Each gesture—a hand placed upon the heart, the low bow of the head—symbolizes a vow: to remain committed to the collective, to forsake the self in service of the whole.

This brief but profound action concludes the ceremony. The initiate, now with their name and helmet, is integrated fully into the Children of the Abandoned. They have shed their past, their old name, and become part of the eternal circle—a shadow within the shadow, forever bound by the unbreakable threads of their collective oath.

With the Naming Ceremony complete, the new member’s life begins anew, for their identity is now aligned with that of the Children of the Abandoned. They wear their new name like a shield, a manifestation of their bond with those who walk the silent path. They have entered the realm where anonymity reigns, and in this profound silence, they begin their journey anew, not as an individual, but as a reflection of the whole.
