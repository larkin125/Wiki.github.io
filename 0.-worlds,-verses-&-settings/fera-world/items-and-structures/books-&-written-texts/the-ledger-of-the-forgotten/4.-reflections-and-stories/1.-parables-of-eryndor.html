1. Parables of Eryndor

Section 4: Parables of Eryndor

The Parables of Eryndor were crafted with a singular purpose in mind: to cultivate virtues that would bind the Children of the Abandoned together in unity. Each parable carries a profound lesson, speaking directly to the heart of what it means to live not for oneself but for the collective. These are not mere stories—they are the very foundation of the Children’s philosophy, lessons to live by, to guide them in times of challenge, doubt, or uncertainty. Eryndor’s wisdom permeates these tales, beckoning each listener to hear the call to selflessness.

Parable 1: The Broken Wheel

"It is the small, silent spokes that make the wheel turn."

In the earliest days of their wanderings, before the Children knew the meaning of true purpose, Eryndor gathered the group beside the warmth of the fire. The flames flickered against the deep chill of the night, casting shadows across their faces, where the light could only catch the edges of their forms. The time had come for a lesson.

They were in a strange land then, traversing through barren fields toward an unknown village. The Children had been pulling a wooden cart, one that Eryndor had built with his own hands. Though rough, it was sturdy, intended to carry them and their meager possessions on their travels.

One fateful evening, when the moon was clouded by shifting skies, Eryndor paused. The cart’s wheel had fractured—a great crack running through it. The weight of the journey had taken its toll, and now they would be forced to abandon the cart if they could not repair it.

Without hesitation, Eryndor addressed the Children. "This wheel is broken, just as we are in many ways. Alone, it is useless, just as a single soul may fall if it stands alone. But we shall not despair. Let us mend the wheel together."

A child named Coris stepped forward, asking, "But the wheel cannot turn with just one spoke, can it? We need every part of it to move forward."

Eryndor smiled and nodded. "True, but it is not the wheel that moves. It is the strength of the hands that carry it, and the unity of those hands that gives it its power. Each spoke—small, silent, humble—supports the wheel. We will all carry it as one. Each of you has something to give, even if you believe it is small. There is no work too insignificant, no hand too weak."

The Children gathered and, one by one, carefully and quietly began to mend the wheel, using wooden boards, nails, and whatever tools they had at their disposal. As the wheel was repaired, the group found that the cart, once broken, was stronger than before. The Children, bound by a simple, unspoken understanding, had proven that it was the unity of effort that brought them strength—not the prowess of a single individual.

From this moment on, it became etched into the very hearts of the Children that their purpose in this world was not to shine as individuals but to be a part of a whole. Without each one, no progress would be made. As Eryndor often said, “The strongest walls are those built by many hands.”

Parable 2: The Broken Mask

"There is no face behind the mask, for the face belongs only to the world."

Years after the Children began their sacred journey, Eryndor saw that the group had become strong, both in numbers and spirit. Yet, not all had embraced the principles of silence and unity that he had taught them. There were those who, in the secret corners of their hearts, still yearned for attention—who wished their individual merits to be recognized.

One day, as they traveled, a child named Elian approached him, bearing in his hands a polished mask—a mask so intricately carved that it gleamed even in the dimmest light. “Master Eryndor,” Elian said, his voice faltering slightly, “this is my face. I offer it to the world. With this mask, I will bring honor to our fellowship. I will do what others cannot.”

Eryndor took the mask carefully in his hands. The surface was decorated with the faces of people—some familiar, others unknown, their expressions captured in eternal permanence. It was a mask of grandeur, meant to showcase strength and pride, to tell the world of Elian’s accomplishments.

“No, Elian,” Eryndor said solemnly, “this mask is not yours to offer. It was given to you by the world, and it is a symbol of all you should leave behind.”

“But what harm does it cause to be known for my strength, to show the world what I can do?” Elian argued.

“Listen to me carefully, child,” Eryndor replied. “There is no face behind the mask, for the world will always seek to label you by what it sees. The mask hides your true self, turning you into something you were never meant to be: an object to be praised or scorned. If you seek the admiration of the world, you will find that it will consume you, just as fire consumes wood. But here, beneath the helmet, your worth is not for the world to judge—it belongs only to the collective. And in this unity, there is a far greater power than any praise can offer.”

Eryndor removed his own helmet and placed it into Elian’s hands. "This is your true face, and with it, you are one with the rest of us. In silence, we find our voice. Let your deeds speak, not your appearance. The world need never know your name. What matters is the strength you give to others, and in giving that strength, you shall find true purpose."

From that moment, Elian understood that the path of unity was not one of shining out but of standing firm within. To seek attention or glory was a temptation that must be cast aside if he was ever to truly find peace with his own heart. The Children of the Abandoned would never reveal their faces to the world, for their identities were forged in the bond between them, not in individual recognition.

Parable 3: The Circle of the Stone

"If we stand together, we are unbreakable."

Long before the Children settled in their first true home, there was a time when they wandered. The night was cold, the moon hidden behind dark clouds, and the path was uncertain. Their strength had already been tested time and again, but this night’s test would be different. The storm was fierce, a tempest of icy winds and roaring thunder. As they struggled forward, it became apparent that they could not continue at the same pace—they needed to find shelter quickly.

Eryndor paused the group and directed their gaze to the mountainside ahead of them. Among the trees, he pointed to a large stone resting at the edge of a precipice. The stone teetered on the edge, as if waiting to tumble into the chasm below.

"This stone is like us," Eryndor said. "Alone, it will fall. But together, we will move it. And together, we shall endure this storm."

The Children, though weary and cold, moved toward the stone, positioning themselves at its sides, each child grasping hold of the rock’s jagged surface. Slowly, patiently, they began to push, their hands working in unison. At first, there was no movement—no shift. But gradually, inch by inch, the stone shifted, as if acknowledging their combined will. When the stone finally lay in a safe place, secure and anchored in the earth, the Children turned toward Eryndor.

"Here is our lesson," he said, his voice firm, but soft against the winds. "This stone, alone, would have fallen and been lost to the world. But when we stand together, we are like the rock: unbreakable, unshakable. It is not our strength, individually, that keeps us whole, but our willingness to stand beside each other, to share the burden, no matter the weight. Together, we find the strength to face all storms."

In the days that followed, the storm passed, but the lesson stayed etched in their hearts: When we are united, no force can topple us. No storm, no hardship, no darkness is too great. In unity, we become unshakable.
